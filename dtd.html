Design Thinking and Doing

A self-directed project to learn more about woodworking design

  • Completed on: November 2018
  • Skills: Solidworks | Woodworking
  • Team Members: Individual
  • Gallery: None

This was my very first design course and I was given the freedom to pursue absolutely anything design-related. Obviously, I chose to build a stool.

However, just building a stool would make it too easy. To make things more difficult, I decide to do everything from scratch – that includes sourcing for materials, designing the stool, cutting the wood to size, assembling the pieces and applying the finishing touches. Having never physically built anything in a workshop prior to this, I thought that it would be easy. I was never so wrong.

I started by creating a CAD to visualise my design. My initial concept was to create dovetail joints, so that no nails or glue would be required to hold the stool together. I brought my concept down to the workshop and it was instantly dismissed – the workshop was not equipped with the appropriate woodworking tools for making such accurate geometric cuts. I learnt my first lesson that day – no matter how fancy the design is, if it cannot be realised practically, it is worthless.

I went back to the drawing board and soon decided to go with a simple square joint instead. With professional advice from the workshop trainers, I sourced for appropriate materials for my wooden stool – cedar dimensional lumber for the legs and oak plywood for the top and the skirts.

After that was where the fun began. I learnt how to operate the various power tools in the shop and got to work preparing all the parts of the stool.

I wrapped up the entire project by sanding the rough edges and applying varnish to the exposed surfaces. It turned out to be so well-made that I used it in my room.

Through this project, I learnt that design and manufacturing goes hand in hand, and it had been a humbling lesson on practicality.
